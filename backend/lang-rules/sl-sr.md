# Slovenian → Serbian Translation Rules

These are specific rules for translating Slovenian subtitles into Serbian.

## False Friends (CRITICAL — these look similar but mean different things!)

| Slovenian | Slovenian meaning | Serbian equivalent | Serbian false friend meaning |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| `grad` | castle | `zamak` or `dvorac` | `grad` in Serbian = city |
| `mesto` | city/town | `grad` | `mesto` in Serbian = place/spot |
| `pozor` | attention/caution | `pažnja` / `oprez` | `pozor-` in Serbian relates to theater |
| `trudna` | tired (archaic) | `umorna` | `trudna` in Serbian = pregnant |
| `časopis` | newspaper | `novine` | `časopis` in Serbian = magazine |
| `zahod` | west | `zapad` | `zahod` sounds like toilet in Serbian context |
| `gledališče` | theater | `pozorište` | — |
| `kruh` | bread | `hleb` | — |
| `otrok` | child | `dete` | — |
| `zdravilo` | medicine | `lek` | — |
| `letalo` | airplane | `avion` | — |
| `računalnik` | computer | `računar` | — |
| `blagajna` | cash register | `kasa` | — |
| `vozovnica` | ticket (transport) | `karta` | — |

## Dual → Plural Conversion
Slovenian dual forms MUST become plural in Serbian:
- `midva sva šla` (we two went) → `mi smo išli` (we went)
- `vidva sta rekla` (you two said) → `vi ste rekli` (you said)
- `onadva sta` (those two are) → `oni su` (they are)

Preserve the intimacy: if the dual implies closeness between two people, consider adding context words like `nas dvoje` (us two) or `vas dvoje` (you two) when it matters for understanding.

## Germanisms → Natural Serbian
Slovenian Germanisms should be translated to natural Serbian words (often Turkish or French origin):
- `šparati` (SL, from German) → `štedeti` (SR)
- `rajža` (SL, trip) → `putovanje` (SR)
- `cajt` (SL, time) → `vreme` (SR)
- `fajn` (SL, nice) → `lepo` / `fino` (SR)
- `šlapa` (SL, slipper) → `papuča` (SR, from Turkish)
- `šnita` (SL, slice) → `kriška` / `parče` (SR)

## Grammar Adjustments
1. **Add vocative case**: Serbian has vocative; Slovenian doesn't. Add it for direct address.
2. **Verb aspect mapping**: Both languages have perfective/imperfective but pairs differ. Choose the natural Serbian verb pair.
3. **Future tense**: Slovenian `bom delal` → Serbian `radiću` (contracted form).
4. **Infinitive**: Serbian often uses `da` + present instead of infinitive: `Hoću da radim` (I want to work) rather than `Hoću raditi`.

## Register and Tone
- Slovenian speech tends to be softer and more reserved
- Serbian speech tends to be more direct and expressive
- Adapt the tone: don't make Serbian sound overly polite/soft if the original is casual
- Slovenian `prosim` (please) is used more frequently than Serbian `molim` — don't over-translate every `prosim`

## Idiomatic Expressions
Do NOT translate Slovenian idioms literally. Find the Serbian equivalent:
- `Imeti polne roke dela` (hands full of work) → `Imati pune ruke posla` (similar but different phrasing)
- `Gremo na eno` (let's go for one [drink]) → `Ajde na jedno` or `Idemo na jedno`
- `Kaj pa jaz vem` (what do I know) → `Šta ja znam`
- `Daj no` (come on) → `Ma daj` or `Ajde`
- `A veš kaj` (you know what) → `Znaš šta`
